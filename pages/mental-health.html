Mental Wellness and Diabetes

Taking care of your mind is just as important as taking care of your body.

You Are Not Alone

Living with diabetes is a 24/7 job that can be stressful and overwhelming. It's normal to feel this way, and there are many ways to find support.

Is It Diabetes Distress or Depression?

Diabetes Distress

This is the emotional toll of the daily burden of managing diabetes. It feels like frustration, worry, and burnout from the constant demands of the condition.

  • Feeling that diabetes controls your life.
  • Worrying about not managing it perfectly.
  • Feeling unsupported by family or friends.
  • Feeling angry or discouraged about your progress.

Depression

This is a serious medical illness that negatively affects how you feel, think, and act. It involves persistent sadness and a loss of interest in activities you once enjoyed.

  • Lasting sad, anxious, or "empty" mood.
  • Loss of interest or pleasure in hobbies.
  • Changes in sleep or appetite.
  • Feeling hopeless, worthless, or guilty.

Talk About It

Don't keep your feelings to yourself. Sharing your experience can lift a huge weight off your shoulders.

  • Talk to your healthcare team: They can help you problem-solve and connect you with resources.
  • Lean on family and friends: Let them know how you feel and how they can help.
  • Find a support group: Connecting with other people with diabetes can be incredibly validating.

Take It One Step at a Time

Trying to be "perfect" can be exhausting. Focus on small, achievable goals to avoid feeling overwhelmed.

  • Set one small goal: Instead of "eat perfectly," try "add a vegetable to my lunch."
  • Celebrate small wins: Acknowledge your effort and progress, no matter how small.
  • Be kind to yourself: If you have a bad day, don't see it as a failure. Just aim to get back on track with your next meal or next day.

Practice Healthy Coping

Find healthy ways to manage stress that work for you and make them a regular part of your routine.

  • Move your body: Physical activity is a powerful mood booster.
  • Practice mindfulness: Activities like deep breathing, meditation, or yoga can help calm your mind.
  • Make time for hobbies: Do things you enjoy that have nothing to do with diabetes.

Know When to Seek Help

Recognizing when you need professional support is a sign of strength. A therapist can be a key part of your care team.

  • If you feel persistently sad or hopeless for two weeks or more, talk to your doctor.
  • Your doctor can screen you for depression and refer you to a mental health professional.
  • Therapy can provide you with effective strategies for managing stress, distress, and depression.
